This article was written during the forced COVID-19 remote-work transition as a practical guide to help teams settle into a new operating norm. The purpose is not to debate COVID-19 itself, but to share actionable guidance for self-regulation and effective remote collaboration so organizations can maintain productivity and keep moving forward.

I have worked remotely for many years. It is challenging, but there are practices that make telework productive and sustainable. Working remotely is not a substitute for in-person collaboration, but virtual teamwork can be highly effective when done with intention.

## Get Up and Get Ready for Work

Follow your normal morning routine: coffee, breakfast, shower, and getting dressed. This helps mentally prepare for the workday.

- Establish office hours.
- Take deliberate breaks.

## Turn On Video Chat

Video conferencing helps teams stay accountable and improves communication quality through nonverbal cues.

If your company does not provide a collaboration platform, there are many free or cost-effective options, including:

- Zoom
- Google Hangouts
- Webex
- Microsoft Teams
- Skype

Regardless of platform, use video when possible.

## Workspace

Use a dedicated workspace away from distractions (TV, chores, and high-traffic home areas).

A formal home office is ideal, but a temporary setup works if it is:

- comfortable
- reasonably quiet
- equipped for focused work

## Stay Connected

Persistent group chat platforms (Slack, Mattermost, Microsoft Teams) are essential for organized communication and institutional memory.

Benefits:

- keeps team conversations centralized
- reduces context loss from scattered email threads
- helps new or absent team members catch up quickly

Non-work channels (family photos, humor, shared interests) also help build trust and team cohesion.

If the team is new to remote work, daily stand-ups are useful for alignment:

- goals for the day
- dependencies and support needed
- anticipated blockers

Keep these meetings short (typically no more than 15 minutes).

## Pick Up the Phone

If a topic requires more than three chat/email exchanges without progress, escalate to a call or video session.

Use ad hoc pairing sessions with screen sharing and virtual whiteboards when needed. Announce the session publicly so others can join, contribute, or learn.

## Dependent Care

Remote work is not a replacement for dependent care.

If childcare or pet-care routines are disrupted, follow company leave/attendance policy. A schedule that protects focused work time is critical.

## Continuous Improvement for Remote Teams

If remote work is new, expect an adaptation period. Weekly retrospectives provide a mechanism for giving and receiving feedback and refining team norms.

## Closing

Remote work strategies should evolve with the team. The goal is not perfection from day one, but intentional habits that support productivity, communication, and trust.
